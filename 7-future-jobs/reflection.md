When I first read the assignment topic — "Future of Software Engineering Jobs" — I thought I already knew the answer. I assumed it would be a simple story: AI is coming, some jobs will disappear, adapt or fall behind. I expected to spend a week gathering data that confirmed something I already vaguely believed.

I was wrong about how I would feel at the end of it.

---

## What Surprised Me Most

The statistic that stopped me was from the McKinsey report: up to 60–70% of software development tasks could be automated by AI. My first reaction was alarm. That is not a small number. But the more I read, the more I understood that "automating tasks" is not the same as "replacing engineers." A surgeon's job involves many tasks — filling paperwork, ordering tests, scheduling — that could be automated. That does not make the surgeon replaceable. The same logic applies here.

What genuinely surprised me was the prediction that software engineering employment is actually expected to *grow* by 25% through 2032, despite everything AI is doing. The world's demand for software is expanding faster than AI can fill it. That was not what I expected to find.

---

## What Concerns Me

I will be honest about what worried me most during this research: the junior developer problem.

I am a student right now. In a few years, I will be trying to enter the industry as a junior engineer. The research I found — especially from experienced engineers like Charity Majors — suggests that the path from junior to senior may become harder, not easier. If AI handles the small tasks that juniors traditionally learn from, there is a real question about how people like me develop real skills and prove our value early in our careers.

This is not an abstract concern for me. It is personal. I am entering a field that is changing faster than any curriculum can track, and the traditional on-ramp into the profession may be getting narrower. That is something I think about seriously.

---

## What Opportunities I See

At the same time, I do not think the picture is only dark for someone starting out today. I think there are real advantages to entering the field *now*, at the moment of transformation, rather than later.

Engineers who grow up alongside AI tools will have a natural relationship with them that older engineers will have to build deliberately. I will not need to "adapt" to AI-assisted development — it will simply be the water I swim in from the beginning. That familiarity is an advantage.

I also see opportunity in the new roles AI is creating. AI integration engineer, MLOps, AI ethics roles — these did not exist ten years ago. Some of the most interesting and well-compensated positions in the next decade will be roles that nobody has fully defined yet. For someone starting now, that is an open space, not a closed door.

---

## Which Skills I Think Will Remain Important

After doing this research, my honest opinion is that the most valuable thing I can do as a student is *not* just learn to write code faster. It is to understand systems, not just syntax. It is to develop the ability to look at a complex problem and understand what is actually being asked — before reaching for a tool to solve it.

Communication matters more than I expected. Several sources I read pointed out that senior engineers spend as much time talking as they do coding — explaining trade-offs, aligning with product managers, justifying architectural decisions. AI cannot do that. A developer who writes decent code and communicates clearly will always have a place. A developer who writes perfect code in isolation may not.

---

## Did My Opinion Change?

Yes, in one important way. I started this research with a vague anxiety about AI in software engineering. I finish it with a more specific understanding of *what* to be worried about and *what* not to be.

I no longer think AI will eliminate software engineering as a career. I do think it will raise the floor — the minimum expected of any engineer — and that the most mechanical and repetitive parts of the job will be handled by machines. That means I need to aim for the parts that machines are bad at: judgment, creativity, communication, and the ability to ask the right questions before writing a single line of code.

---

## How I Will Personally Adapt

My plan going forward is:

1. **Build a strong conceptual foundation.** Not just in how to use frameworks, but in how systems work underneath. Data structures, algorithms, networking, databases — things that do not change just because the tools do.

2. **Learn to work *with* AI tools, not around them.** Using Copilot, understanding prompt engineering, knowing when to trust AI output and when to question it — this is a practical skill I need to develop alongside everything else.

3. **Focus on communication.** I want to practice explaining technical ideas clearly, to technical and non-technical audiences. This is something I can work on right now, before I even start a professional role.

4. **Stay curious about the field itself.** The engineers who will struggle in the AI era are those who stop paying attention. I want to keep reading, keep experimenting, and keep asking questions — even when the answers are uncomfortable.

This assignment asked me to think about the future of my own career before I have even properly started it. That felt strange at first. But I think it is exactly the right question to be asking at this moment.
